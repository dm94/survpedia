Gasoline (or petrol) is a transparent, petroleum-derived flammable liquid that is used primarily as a fuel in spark-ignited internal combustion engines.

## Description of what it is like

Gasoline is a clear or slightly yellowish liquid with a distinctive, pungent odor. It is highly volatile, meaning it evaporates quickly at room temperature, forming a heavy, flammable vapor that can travel along the ground.

## Origin and where to find it

- **Refinery Product**: It is not found in nature; it must be refined from **[Crude Oil](/en/materials/crude-oil)**.
- **Distillation**: The process involves heating crude oil in a distillation column to separate its components by their boiling points.

### Minimum processing required

- **[Distillation](/en/inventions/distillation)**: To separate the gasoline range hydrocarbons (boiling between 30°C and 200°C) from the rest of the crude oil.
- **Purification**: Removing sulfur and other impurities that can damage engines or cause excessive pollution.
- **Additives**: Modern gasoline includes chemicals to prevent engine knocking and improve storage life.

### Tools needed to work on it

- **Fractional Distillation Column**: A tall, temperature-controlled tower.
- **Condenser**: To turn the hot vapors back into liquid.
- **Airtight Containers**: For safe storage and to prevent evaporation.

### Common forms of use

- **Automotive Fuel**: The most common use, powering light vehicles.
- **Aviation Gasoline (Avgas)**: High-octane fuel for piston-engine aircraft.
- **White Gas**: A highly refined form used in camping stoves and lanterns.

### Possible substitutes

- **[Alcohol](/en/materials/alcohol)** (Ethanol): Can be used as a fuel, though it has lower energy density and may require engine modifications.
- **[Biofuel](/en/materials/biofuel)**: Renewable alternatives derived from plants.
- **[Propane](/en/materials/propane)**: A gaseous fuel that can be compressed into a liquid for use in engines.
- **[Wood Gasifier](/en/inventions/wood-gasifier)**: Allows engines to run on gases produced from wood or charcoal.

### Limitations and common failures

- **Volatility**: It evaporates quickly if not kept in a sealed container, losing its ability to start engines easily.
- **Degradation**: Over time (3-6 months), gasoline oxidizes and forms gummy deposits that can clog carburetors and fuel injectors.
- **Water Sensitivity**: Water in gasoline can cause engine sputtering, corrosion, and ice blockages in cold weather.

### Risks and safety

- **Extreme Fire Hazard**: Vapors are highly explosive; keep far away from sparks or flames.
- **Toxicity**: Harmful if swallowed or inhaled; can cause skin irritation.
- **Environmental Hazard**: Spills are toxic to plants and animals and can contaminate groundwater.
