Methanol, also known as wood alcohol, is the simplest alcohol (CH3OH).

## Description of what it is like

A colorless, volatile, flammable liquid with a distinctive alcoholic odor. It is miscible with water.

## Origin and where to find it

- Not found free in nature in significant amounts.
- Obtained from the distillation of wood in the absence of air. See **[Wood Distillation](/en/knowledge/wood-distillation)**.

### Minimum processing required

- Destructive distillation of wood.
- Fractional distillation to separate it from water, acetic acid, and acetone.

### Tools needed to work on it

- **[Alembic](/en/inventions/alembic)** or distillation column.
- Airtight storage containers.

### Common forms of use

- **Fuel**: Can be used as a high-octane fuel or fuel additive.
- **Solvent**: Widely used in industrial processes.
- **Antifreeze**: Due to its low freezing point.

### Possible substitutes

- **[Ethanol](/en/materials/alcohol)**: Grain alcohol, less toxic and also used as fuel/solvent.
- **[Acetone](/en/materials/acetone)**: For some solvent applications.

### Limitations and common failures

- Toxicity: Extremely dangerous if ingested or inhaled.
- Corrosivity: Can attack some plastics and metals (like aluminum) if used as fuel.

### Risks and safety

- Blindness and Death: Small amounts can cause permanent blindness or death if swallowed.
- Flame Visibility: Burns with a nearly invisible blue flame, making fires hard to detect in daylight.
