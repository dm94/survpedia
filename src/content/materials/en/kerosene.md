Kerosene (also known as paraffin or lamp oil) is a combustible hydrocarbon liquid derived from petroleum. It is widely used as a fuel in industry and households.

## Description of what it is like

Kerosene is typically a clear, thin liquid with a characteristic odor. It is less volatile than gasoline, meaning it does not evaporate as quickly at room temperature, which makes it safer to store and handle for lighting and heating.

## Origin and where to find it

- **Refinery Product**: Extracted from **[Crude Oil](/en/materials/crude-oil)** through refining.
- **Historic Sources**: Historically also produced from coal, oil shale, and wood (through more complex processes).

### Minimum processing required

- **[Distillation](/en/inventions/distillation)**: Fractional distillation of crude oil to isolate the kerosene fraction.
- **Treatment**: Often treated with solvents or acid to remove sulfur and unsaturated hydrocarbons, which reduces smoke and odor when burned.
- **Filtration**: To remove any particulate matter.

### Tools needed to work on it

- **Fractional Distillation Column**: To separate it from crude oil.
- **Condenser**: To liquefy the vapors.
- **Storage Tanks**: Typically metal or high-density plastic.

### Common forms of use

- **Lighting**: Used in hurricane lamps and pressure lamps (like Petromax).
- **Heating**: Portable kerosene heaters for indoor use.
- **Cooking**: Kerosene stoves (wick or pressure types).
- **Aviation**: Jet A-1 fuel is a highly specialized and refined form of kerosene.

### Possible substitutes

- **[Vegetable Oil](/en/materials/vegetable-oil)**: Can be used in simple wick lamps, though it burns less brightly and can clog wicks.
- **[Animal Fat](/en/materials/animal-fat)** (Tallow): Used in candles and very basic lamps.
- **[Biofuel](/en/materials/biofuel)**: Specifically biodiesel or bio-kerosene derived from plant oils.
- **[Gasoline](/en/materials/gasoline)**: NOT a safe substitute for lamps or heaters designed for kerosene due to its high volatility and explosion risk.

### Limitations and common failures

- **Wick Fouling**: Impure kerosene can leave carbon deposits on wicks, causing smoke and reduced light.
- **Water Contamination**: Like other fuels, water can cause issues in burners and promote corrosion.
- **Odor**: When burned in poorly maintained equipment, it can release a strong, unpleasant smell.

### Risks and safety

- **Fire Hazard**: While safer than gasoline, it is still highly flammable.
- **Carbon Monoxide**: Burning kerosene in unventilated spaces can lead to a dangerous buildup of CO.
- **Poisoning**: Toxic if swallowed or inhaled; a common cause of accidental poisoning in children.
