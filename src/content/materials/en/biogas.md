Biogas is a mixture of different gases produced by the breakdown of organic matter in the absence of oxygen.

## Description of what it is like

It is a colorless gas, primarily composed of methane (CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2). It may have a slight odor of rotten eggs if hydrogen sulfide (H2S) is present.

## Origin and where to find it

- Produced in swamps (marsh gas), landfills, and through the digestion process in animals.
- Artificially produced in a **[Biogas Digester](/en/inventions/biogas-digester)** using organic waste.

### Minimum processing required

- Collection from an anaerobic environment.
- Scrubbing (removal of H2S and moisture) is recommended for use in engines.

### Tools needed to work on it

- Airtight containers for collection.
- **[Pipes](/en/inventions/pipes)** and **[Valves](/en/inventions/valve)** for transport.
- Gas storage bags or tanks.

### Common forms of use

- **Direct Combustion**: Burned in stoves or lamps.
- **Power Generation**: Used in modified internal combustion engines to drive generators.

### Possible substitutes

- **[Natural Gas](/en/materials/crude-oil)**: Fossil equivalent.
- **[Propane](/en/materials/propane)**: More energy-dense but requires industrial production.
- **[Wood Gas](/en/inventions/wood-gasifier)**: Produced by gasification of wood.

### Limitations and common failures

- Low Energy Density: Contains less energy per volume than natural gas.
- Storage: Difficult to compress and store in large quantities without specialized equipment.

### Risks and safety

- Asphyxiation: Can displace oxygen in enclosed spaces.
- Fire/Explosion: Highly flammable when mixed with air.
