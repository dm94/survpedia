Biogas is a mixture of gases, primarily methane and carbon dioxide, produced by the breakdown of organic matter in the absence of oxygen. It is a renewable energy source that can be produced from raw materials such as agricultural waste, manure, municipal waste, plant material, sewage, green waste, or food waste.

## Description of what it is like

Biogas is a colorless and odorless gas when pure, though it often has a slight pungent smell due to trace amounts of hydrogen sulfide and other impurities. It burns with a clean blue flame, similar to natural gas.

## Origin and where to find it

- **Environments**: Swamps, marshes, sewage systems, and specialized digestion tanks.
- **Signs**: Bubbles rising from stagnant, organic-rich water; characteristic smell of decomposition in anaerobic environments.
- **Natural/Synthetic**: Produced naturally in anaerobic environments, but captured synthetically for use as fuel.

### Minimum processing required

1.  **Collection**: Gathering organic waste (manure, food scraps, etc.).
2.  **Digestion**: Placing the waste in a sealed, oxygen-free container (digester) with anaerobic bacteria.
3.  **Filtration**: Removing water vapor and hydrogen sulfide to prevent corrosion of equipment.
4.  **Storage**: Collecting the gas in a flexible or rigid container for later use.

### Tools needed to work on it

- **Digester**: A sealed tank or pit.
- **Pipes**: For transporting the gas.
- **Gas Scrubber**: Simple filters (like iron filings or water) to remove impurities.
- **Storage Tank**: To hold the pressurized or unpressurized gas.

### Common forms of use

- **Fuel Gas**: Burned directly in stoves or heaters.
- **Power Generation**: Used in internal combustion engines to drive generators.
- **Biomethane**: Purified biogas (methane-rich) that can be injected into natural gas grids.

### Possible substitutes

- **Natural Gas**: Fossil fuel equivalent.
- **Wood Gas**: Produced by gasifying wood (requires high heat, not biological digestion).
- **Propane/Butane**: Bottled petroleum gases.

### Limitations and common failures

- **Temperature Sensitivity**: Anaerobic bacteria require warm temperatures (ideally 35-40°C) to produce gas efficiently.
- **pH Balance**: If the digester becomes too acidic, the bacteria will die.
- **Explosion Risk**: Like any flammable gas, it can explode if mixed with air and ignited in a confined space.
- **Corrosion**: Impurities like hydrogen sulfide can corrode metal pipes and engines.

### Risks and safety

- **Asphyxiation**: In high concentrations, it can displace oxygen in enclosed spaces.
- **Fire/Explosion**: Requires careful handling of leaks and ignition sources.
- **Pathogens**: Raw organic waste used for production can contain harmful bacteria.
