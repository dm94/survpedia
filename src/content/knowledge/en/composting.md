# Brief description

Composting is the natural process of recycling organic matter, such as leaves and food scraps, into a valuable fertilizer that can enrich soil and plants. It is a cornerstone of sustainable agriculture, transforming waste into a vital resource for soil health.

## Use / Function

- **Soil Amendment:** Improves soil structure, aeration, and water-holding capacity.
- **Fertilization:** Provides a slow-release source of essential nutrients (Nitrogen, Phosphorus, Potassium) to crops.
- **Waste Management:** Significantly reduces the volume of organic waste in a settlement.
- **Disease Suppression:** Helps plants resist common soil-borne pests and diseases.

## Operating principle

Composting is an aerobic (oxygen-requiring) biological process where microorganisms (bacteria, fungi) and larger organisms (earthworms, insects) decompose organic matter.

1.  **Carbon/Nitrogen Balance**: Requires a mix of "browns" (carbon-rich materials like dry leaves or straw) and "greens" (nitrogen-rich materials like food scraps or fresh grass).
2.  **Microbial Activity**: Microbes consume the organic matter, using carbon for energy and nitrogen for protein synthesis.
3.  **Thermophilic Phase**: In a well-constructed pile, microbial activity generates enough heat (up to 60-70°C) to kill most pathogens and weed seeds.
4.  **Maturation**: As the process slows, the material is transformed into humus, a stable organic substance.

## How to create it

1.  **Select a Site**: Choose a well-drained, accessible area, preferably in partial shade.
2.  **Build the Pile**: Start with a layer of coarse "browns" (like twigs or straw) for drainage and aeration.
3.  **Layering**: Alternate layers of "greens" and "browns". A rough ratio of 3 parts brown to 1 part green is ideal.
4.  **Add Moisture**: The pile should be as damp as a wrung-out sponge. Add water during construction and periodically thereafter.
5.  **Aeration (Turning)**: Use a fork or shovel to turn the pile every week or two. This provides oxygen to the microbes and moves outer material to the hot center.
6.  **Curing**: Once the pile no longer heats up after turning, let it sit for several weeks to finish the decomposition process.

## Materials needed

- **Essential Browns**: **[Straw](/en/materials/straw)**, dry leaves, shredded bark, or sawdust.
- **Essential Greens**: Vegetable and fruit scraps, grass clippings, or herbivore manure.
- **Water**: Essential for microbial life.
- **Tools**:
    - **[Shovel](/en/inventions/shovel)** or pitchfork for turning.
    - Enclosure: Can be built using **[Wood](/en/materials/wood)** or **[Stone](/en/materials/stone)** to keep the pile contained and retain heat.

## Variants and improvements

- **Vermicomposting**: Utilizing specific earthworms (like Red Wigglers) to accelerate decomposition, producing high-quality "worm castings".
- **Hot Composting**: A high-maintenance method involving precise C:N ratios and frequent turning to produce compost in as little as 18 days.
- **Composting Latrine**: Specialized **[Latrine](/en/inventions/latrine)** designs that safely transform human waste into compost over a long period.

## Limits and risks

- **Odor Issues**: A pile that is too wet or lacks oxygen will turn anaerobic and produce foul smells.
- **Pests**: Exposed food scraps can attract rodents or insects; burying "greens" under "browns" helps prevent this.
- **Chemical Contamination**: Avoid composting materials treated with persistent herbicides or pesticides.
- **Pathogens**: If using manure, ensure the pile reaches high temperatures to kill potential pathogens.
- **Nutrient Leaching**: If left uncovered in heavy rain, valuable nutrients can be washed out of the pile.
