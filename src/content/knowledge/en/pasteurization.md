# Brief description

Pasteurization is a heat-treatment process that destroys pathogenic microorganisms in certain foods and beverages. It is named after the French scientist Louis Pasteur, who discovered in the 1860s that thermal processing could kill spoilage organisms.

## Use / Function

Its primary purpose is food safety and shelf-life extension:

- **Pathogen Destruction:** Eliminates bacteria such as Salmonella, E. coli, and Listeria.
- **Spoilage Prevention:** Kills yeasts and molds that cause food to sour or rot.
- **Public Health:** Significantly reduces the transmission of diseases like tuberculosis and brucellosis through milk.
- **Preservation:** Extends the period during which food remains safe to consume.

## Operating principle

Pasteurization works on the principle of thermal death: microbes have a specific temperature at which their essential proteins and enzymes denature, killing them.

1. **Heating:** The liquid (typically milk, juice, or beer) is heated to a specific temperature below its boiling point.
2. **Holding:** The liquid is maintained at that temperature for a predetermined period.
3. **Rapid Cooling:** The liquid is cooled quickly to prevent the growth of any surviving spores and to preserve flavor.

## How to create it

For a basic "batch" pasteurization (LTLT - Low Temperature Long Time):
1. **Container:** Place the liquid in a clean **[glass](/en/materials/glass)** or metal container.
2. **Water Bath:** Place the container in a larger pot of **[water](/en/materials/water)** (double boiler) to ensure even heating and prevent scorching.
3. **Heating:** Heat the water until the liquid inside reaches 63°C (145°F).
4. **Timer:** Hold the liquid at exactly 63°C for 30 minutes. Use a **[thermometer](/en/inventions/thermometer)** for precision.
5. **Cooling:** Immediately place the container in ice water to cool it down rapidly.

## Materials needed

- **Liquid to treat:** Milk, juice, beer, or vinegar.
- **Heating source:** Fire or stove.
- **Tools:**
  - **[Thermometer](/en/inventions/thermometer)**: Essential for ensuring the correct temperature is reached without boiling.
  - **[Glass](/en/materials/glass)** bottles or jars.
  - Pot for **[water](/en/materials/water)** bath.

## Variants and improvements

- **LTLT (Batch):** 63°C for 30 minutes. Best for small-scale/home use.
- **HTST (High Temperature Short Time):** 72°C for 15 seconds. The most common commercial method (Flash pasteurization).
- **UHT (Ultra-High Temperature):** 135°C for 2-5 seconds. Makes milk shelf-stable for months without refrigeration (affects flavor).
- **Vat Pasteurization:** Large scale version of the batch method.

## Limits and risks

- **Not Sterilization:** Pasteurization does not kill all microorganisms (like bacterial spores). Refrigeration is still required for most pasteurized foods.
- **Flavor Change:** Excessive heat can "cook" the food, changing its taste and nutritional profile.
- **Precision Required:** If the temperature is too low or the time too short, pathogens may survive. If too high, the product may be ruined.
- **Recontamination:** If the containers are not sterile or handled properly after heating, the food can be contaminated again.
