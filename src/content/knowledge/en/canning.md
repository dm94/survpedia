# Brief description

Canning is a method of preserving food in which the food contents are processed and sealed in an airtight container (jars like Mason jars, or steel and tin cans). Canning provides a shelf life that typically ranges from one to five years, although under specific circumstances, it can be much longer.

## Use / Function

Its primary purpose is long-term food storage without refrigeration:

- **Food Security:** Storing harvests for use during winter or periods of scarcity.
- **Logistics:** Allowing food to be transported over long distances without spoiling.
- **Convenience:** Ready-to-eat food that requires minimal preparation.
- **Waste Reduction:** Preserving surplus produce that would otherwise rot.

## Operating principle

Canning works by combining heat sterilization with an airtight seal:

1. **Preparation:** Food is cleaned, often cooked or blanched, and placed in containers.
2. **Heating:** The filled containers are heated to a temperature that destroys spoilage microorganisms and inactivates enzymes.
3. **Sealing:** As the container cools, a vacuum seal is formed, preventing new microorganisms from entering.
4. **Acidity Factor:** High-acid foods (fruits, tomatoes) can be processed in boiling water, while low-acid foods (meats, vegetables) require higher temperatures achieved via a pressure canner to kill *Clostridium botulinum* spores.

## How to create it

### Water Bath Method (For High-Acid Foods)
1. **Sanitize:** Boil **[glass](/en/materials/glass)** jars and lids in **[water](/en/materials/water)** to sterilize them.
2. **Fill:** Pack the food into the jars, leaving a small amount of "headspace" at the top.
3. **Seal:** Wipe the rims and apply the lids and bands.
4. **Process:** Submerge the jars in a pot of boiling **[water](/en/materials/water)**. The time varies by food type (usually 10-45 minutes).
5. **Cool:** Remove jars and let them cool undisturbed. You should hear a "pop" as the vacuum seal forms.

### Pressure Canning (For Low-Acid Foods)
1. Follow the same filling steps.
2. Place jars in a specialized pressure vessel.
3. Heat until the internal pressure reaches 10-15 psi (reaching approx. 116-121°C).
4. Maintain pressure for the required time (usually 60-90 minutes).

## Materials needed

- **Containers:** **[Glass](/en/materials/glass)** jars with rubber-sealed lids or **[tin](/en/materials/tin)**-plated steel cans.
- **Preservative:** **[Water](/en/materials/water)**, brine (salt), or syrup (sugar).
- **Tools:**
  - Large pot (Water bath) or Pressure Canner.
  - Jar lifter and funnel.
  - Heating source (Fire/Stove).

## Variants and improvements

- **Appertization:** The original method using glass jars sealed with wax and wire, developed by Nicolas Appert.
- **Tin Cans:** Developed later for durability and ease of transport, especially for military use.
- **Aseptic Packaging:** Modern method where food and container are sterilized separately and then joined (e.g., Tetra Pak).
- **Home Canning:** Using specialized jars (like Mason or Weck jars) that are reusable.

## Limits and risks

- **Botulism:** The most serious risk. Low-acid foods not processed in a pressure canner can harbor *Clostridium botulinum*, which produces a deadly toxin in anaerobic conditions.
- **Seal Failure:** If the seal is broken, the food will spoil rapidly. Always check if the lid is concave and firm.
- **Nutrient Loss:** High heat can destroy some vitamins (like Vitamin C and B-complex).
- **Corrosion:** **[Tin](/en/materials/tin)** or steel cans can rust if stored in damp conditions.
