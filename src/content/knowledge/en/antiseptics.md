# Brief description

Antiseptics are antimicrobial substances applied to living tissue or skin to reduce the possibility of infection, sepsis, or putrefaction. Unlike antibiotics, which kill bacteria inside the body, antiseptics are used topically to sanitize wounds, surgical sites, and hands.

## Use / Function

- **Wound Care**: Disinfecting cuts, scrapes, and burns to prevent localized and systemic infections (sepsis).
- **Surgical Preparation**: Sanitizing the skin of both patient and surgeon before incisions are made.
- **Sanitation**: Cleaning skin after contact with infectious agents or contaminated materials.
- **Scale**: Domestic, clinical, and surgical applications.

## Operating principle

Antiseptics function by disrupting the physical structure or chemical processes of microbial cells (bacteria, fungi, and viruses):

1. **Membrane Disruption**: Solvents like alcohols dissolve the lipid membrane of cells, causing cellular contents to leak.
2. **Protein Denaturation**: Active agents unfold the complex protein structures of microbes, rendering vital enzymes inactive.
3. **Oxidation**: Strong oxidizers (like chlorine or hydrogen peroxide) strip electrons from molecular structures, breaking chemical bonds and destroying cellular components.

## How to implement

Using the correct antiseptic for the right task is crucial:

### 1. Alcohol Solutions
- Use **[Alcohol](/en/materials/alcohol)** (ethanol or isopropyl alcohol) at a concentration of 60% to 80% with water. Pure 100% alcohol is less effective because water is necessary to help denature proteins and penetrate cell walls.

### 2. Phenol / Carbolic Acid
- Use highly diluted **[Phenol](/en/materials/phenol)** (0.5% to 1.0%) for surgical environments. Phenol was the first widespread antiseptic introduced by Joseph Lister, though it is harsh on tissues.

### 3. Chlorine / Bleach
- Use very low concentrations of **[Bleach](/en/materials/bleach)** (sodium hypochlorite) diluted in water (e.g., Dakin's solution, around 0.5%) for cleaning dirty, infected wounds when other alternatives are unavailable. Strong bleach must *never* be used on wounds as it destroys healthy tissue.

## Materials needed

- **Alcohols**: Sourced from fermentation and fractional distillation of grain or starch.
- **Phenol**: Sourced from coal tar distillation or processed from benzene.
- **Hypochlorites**: Created by reacting chlorine gas with sodium hydroxide, or through simple electrolysis of salt water.
- **Clean Water**: Essential for dilution to safe, effective concentrations.

## Variants and improvements

- **Iodine/Povidone-Iodine**: A highly effective, broad-spectrum antiseptic that is less irritating than pure iodine tincture.
- **Hydrogen Peroxide**: A mild oxidizing antiseptic used to mechanically clean wounds through effervescence (bubbling).
- **Chlorhexidine**: A modern, highly effective antiseptic with long-lasting activity on the skin, widely used in modern surgeries.

## Limits and risks

- **Tissue Toxicity**: Strong antiseptics can damage healthy human cells and delay wound healing. They should be used at correct, safe dilutions.
- **Incomplete Sterilization**: Antiseptics reduce the number of microbes but do not achieve absolute sterilization (which is done via an **[Autoclave](/en/inventions/autoclave)** on inert objects).
- **Allergic Reactions**: Some agents (especially iodine) can trigger allergic contact dermatitis.
- **Chemical Burns**: Concentrated phenols, bleach, or alcohols can cause chemical burns if left on tissue.
