# Brief description

A hand plane is a tool for shaping wood using muscle power to force the cutting blade over the wood surface. It is used to flatten, reduce the thickness of, and impart a smooth surface to a rough piece of lumber.

## Use / Function

Its practical purpose:
- Flattening rough wood surfaces.
- Squaring edges of boards.
- Adjusting the thickness of a piece of wood.
- Creating smooth finishes that may not require sanding.

## Operating principle

A sharp metal blade (the iron) is held at a fixed angle within a body (the stock). As the plane is pushed or pulled across the wood, the blade slices off a thin layer of wood (a shaving), which is guided away by the mouth of the plane. The flat sole of the plane ensures that the blade only cuts high spots, eventually creating a perfectly flat surface.

## How to create it

- **Minimum functional version**: A block of hard wood with a rectangular hole (mortise) cut at an angle (usually 45 degrees). A steel blade is wedged into this hole.
- **Essential**: A very sharp steel blade and a perfectly flat sole.
- **Technical level**: Intermediate (requires precise woodworking to seat the blade correctly).

## Materials needed

- **Essential materials**: Hard **[Wood](/en/materials/wood)** (for the body), high-carbon **[Steel](/en/materials/steel)** (for the blade).
- **Tools**: **[Chisel](/en/inventions/chisel)**, **[Saw](/en/inventions/saw)**, **[Hammer](/en/inventions/hammer)**.
- **Possible substitutes**: Cast **[Iron](/en/materials/iron)** can be used for the body in more advanced versions.

## Variants and improvements

- **Wooden Planes**: Traditional style where the blade is held by a wooden wedge.
- **Scrub Plane**: Used for rapid removal of large amounts of wood.
- **Jack Plane**: A versatile, mid-sized plane for general smoothing and flattening.
- **Jointer Plane**: Long plane used for making edges perfectly straight.
- **Metal Planes**: Use a screw-based adjustment mechanism for finer control.

## Limits and risks

- **Maintenance**: The blade must be kept extremely sharp; a dull blade will tear the wood fibers.
- **Grain direction**: Planning against the grain can cause "tear-out."
- **Safety**: The blade is exposed and extremely sharp. Keep fingers away from the mouth.
