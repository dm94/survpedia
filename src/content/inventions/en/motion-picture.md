# Brief description

A motion picture is a series of still images which, when shown on a screen in rapid succession, create the illusion of moving images. This phenomenon is due to persistence of vision and the phi phenomenon. It is the basis for cinema and modern video technology.

## Use / Function

- **Entertainment:** Storytelling, artistic expression, and mass leisure.
- **Education:** Visual documentation of scientific processes and historical events.
- **Communication:** Disseminating information and propaganda.
- **Surveillance:** Recording events for security and analysis.

## Operating principle

The creation and display of motion pictures rely on several key stages:

1.  **Capture (Camera):** A camera exposes a series of photographic frames on a light-sensitive film strip at a constant rate (e.g., 24 frames per second).
2.  **Intermittent Motion:** A mechanism (like a Geneva drive) pulls the film forward frame by frame, stopping it momentarily for exposure or projection.
3.  **Persistence of Vision:** The human brain retains an image for a fraction of a second. If images change fast enough, the brain blends them together.
4.  **Projection:** A powerful light source shines through the developed film, and a **[Lens](/en/inventions/lens)** projects the image onto a screen. A shutter blocks the light while the film is moving between frames to prevent blurring.

## How to create it

Building a basic motion picture system requires advanced mechanics and chemistry:

- **Camera/Projector Mechanism:** An intermittent movement device (Geneva drive or claw mechanism) to move film precisely.
- **Optical System:** High-quality **[Lenses](/en/inventions/lens)** for focusing images onto film and projecting them.
- **Film Base:** A flexible, transparent strip (originally nitrocellulose, later acetate or polyester).
- **Emulsion:** A light-sensitive coating of silver halide crystals in gelatin.
- **Light Source:** An **[Arc Lamp](/en/inventions/arc-lamp)** or high-intensity incandescent bulb for projection.

## Materials needed

- **Film Strip:** **[Plastic](/en/materials/plastic)** (acetate or polyester).
- **Sensitivity:** **[Silver](/en/materials/silver)** (silver nitrate/halides) for the emulsion.
- **Optics:** **[Glass](/en/materials/glass)** for lenses and prisms.
- **Housing:** **[Steel](/en/materials/steel)** or **[Aluminum](/en/materials/aluminum)** for the camera and projector bodies.
- **Chemistry:** Various chemicals for developing and fixing the photographic image.

## Variants and improvements

- **Kinetoscope:** An early peep-hole viewer for individual use.
- **Cinematograph:** A combined camera, printer, and projector (Lumière brothers).
- **Sound Film (Talkies):** Adding a synchronized audio track (optical or magnetic) to the film strip.
- **Color Film:** Using multiple layers of emulsion to capture red, green, and blue light.
- **Digital Cinema:** Replacing physical film with digital sensors and projectors.

## Limits and risks

- **Flammability:** Early "nitrate" film was highly flammable and could explode if stored improperly.
- **Mechanical Wear:** Film is easily scratched, torn, or degraded by heat and humidity.
- **Complexity:** Requires precise synchronization between film movement and shutter timing.
- **Cost:** Producing and developing physical film is expensive compared to digital methods.
