# Brief description

A mechanical part able to convert between reciprocating motion and rotational motion. It is a central component in many engines and machines.

## Use / Function

- **Primary use**: Converting linear motion of pistons into rotational motion for wheels or pulleys.
- **Secondary uses**: Driving pumps, sawmills, and traditional flour mills.
- **Scale**: Industrial/Mechanical.

## Operating principle

The crankshaft consists of a series of "cranks" or "throws" that are offset from the main axis of rotation.
1. **Reciprocating Input**: A connecting rod attached to a piston pushes on a crank pin.
2. **Torque Generation**: Because the pin is offset from the center, the force creates a turning moment (torque).
3. **Rotational Output**: The entire shaft rotates, providing power to other components.

## How to create it

- **Level**: Advanced.
- **Manufacturing**: In simple forms, it can be forged from a single piece of iron. In industrial settings, it is precisely machined from high-strength steel.
- **Balance**: Must be carefully balanced with counterweights to prevent vibration at high speeds.

## Materials needed

- **Essential materials**: Steel or Iron (must be strong enough to withstand bending and twisting forces).
- **Tools**: Forge, Lathe, Milling machine.

## Variants and improvements

- **Single-throw Crank**: Used in simple machines like a foot-powered lathe.
- **Multi-throw Crank**: Used in multi-cylinder engines for smoother power delivery.

## Limits and risks

- **Fatigue**: Constant stress can lead to cracks and eventual failure.
- **Lubrication**: Critical to prevent wear on the main bearings and crank pins.
- **Complexity**: Precision machining is required for reliable operation in high-speed applications.
