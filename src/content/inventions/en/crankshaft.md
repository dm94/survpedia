# Brief description

A crankshaft is a mechanical part able to perform a conversion between reciprocating motion and rotational motion. In a reciprocating engine, it translates reciprocating motion of the piston into rotational motion, whereas in a reciprocating compressor, it converts the rotational motion into reciprocating motion.

## Use / Function

- **Engines:** The core of internal combustion and steam engines, converting piston power to wheel or propeller rotation.
- **Pumps and Compressors:** Converting motor rotation into the back-and-forth pumping action.
- **Manual Tools:** Hand-powered drills (braces) and winches.
- **Scale:** From tiny model engines to massive ship engines.

## Operating principle

The crankshaft consists of a series of cranks and crankpins to which the connecting rods are attached.
- **Offset:** The crankpins are offset from the main axis of rotation.
- **Leverage:** The connecting rod pushes or pulls on the crankpin, creating a moment (torque) around the main axis.
- **Conversion:** As the piston moves up and down, the connecting rod follows the circular path of the crankpin, resulting in continuous rotation.

## How to create it

1.  **Main Shaft:** A central rotating shaft supported by main bearings.
2.  **Crank Arms (Webs):** Arms extending out from the main shaft.
3.  **Crankpins:** Off-center pins where the connecting rods attach.
4.  **Counterweights:** Weights opposite the crankpins to balance the rotating mass and reduce vibration.
5.  **Manufacturing:**
    - **Forging:** For high strength, a single piece of steel is hammered into shape.
    - **Casting:** For complex shapes or lower stress applications, using cast iron.
    - **Assembly:** For very large engines, separate parts are shrunk-fit together.

## Materials needed

- **Main Body:** **[Steel](/en/materials/steel)** (forged for high strength) or **[Cast Iron](/en/materials/cast-iron)**.
- **Bearings:** **[Bronze](/en/materials/bronze)** or white metal (babbitt) to reduce friction and wear.
- **Lubrication:** Essential oil or grease to prevent metal-to-metal contact.

## Variants and improvements

- **Single Crank:** Used in simple engines or manual tools.
- **Multi-cylinder Crankshaft:** Multiple cranks set at specific angles (e.g., 180°, 120°, 90°) to balance power strokes and reduce vibration.
- **Built-up Crankshaft:** Made of separate components joined together.

## Limits and risks

- **Fatigue:** Constant stress cycles can cause microscopic cracks leading to sudden failure.
- **Vibration:** Improperly balanced crankshafts can shake an engine to pieces at high speeds (torsional vibration).
- **Lubrication Failure:** Without oil, the bearings will overheat and "seize," destroying the shaft.
- **Alignment:** Must be perfectly straight; even a small bend will cause rapid wear and failure.
