# Brief description

A syringe is a simple reciprocating pump consisting of a plunger that fits tightly within a cylindrical tube called a barrel. It is used to inject or withdraw fluids, and is essential for precise administration of medicine, vaccination, and scientific sampling.

## Use / Function

Its practical purpose:
- Precise administration of liquid medicines and vaccines.
- Withdrawal of blood or other fluids for testing.
- Irrigation of wounds or orifices.
- Precision application of lubricants or adhesives in technical work.

## Operating principle

The syringe operates on the principle of atmospheric pressure and vacuum. When the plunger is pulled back, it creates a vacuum inside the barrel, which draws liquid in through the orifice. When the plunger is pushed forward, it creates pressure that forces the liquid out. The tight seal between the plunger and the barrel (often aided by a rubber gasket) is crucial to maintain this pressure differential.

## How to create it

- **Minimum functional version**: A hollowed-out tube (like a glass pipe) with a piston made of wood or metal wrapped in leather or rubber to create a seal.
- **Essential**: A perfectly cylindrical and smooth barrel, and a plunger that forms an airtight seal.
- **Technical level**: High (requires precision machining or glassblowing to ensure a tight fit and avoid leaks).

## Materials needed

- **Essential materials**: **[Glass](/en/materials/glass)** or transparent plastic (for the barrel), stainless **[Steel](/en/materials/steel)** (for the needle), and **[Natural Rubber](/en/materials/natural-rubber)** (for the plunger gasket).
- **Possible substitutes**: **[Leather](/en/materials/leather)** can be used for the plunger seal in primitive versions.

## Variants and improvements

- **Glass Syringes**: Reusable and can be sterilized by boiling or autoclaving.
- **Hypodermic Needles**: Hollow needles made of steel used for injections under the skin.
- **Luer-Lock**: A standardized screw-connection system to prevent needles from detaching under pressure.
- **Auto-injectors**: Spring-loaded devices for rapid or emergency self-administration.

## Limits and risks

- **Sterility**: The single most important factor. Non-sterile syringes can transmit deadly diseases (like sepsis, HIV, or hepatitis).
- **Air Embolism**: Injecting air into the bloodstream can be fatal.
- **Fragility**: Glass syringes are prone to breaking and causing injury.
- **Precision**: Manufacturing must be precise to ensure accurate dosage.
