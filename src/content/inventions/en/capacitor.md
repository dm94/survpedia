# Brief description

A capacitor is a device that stores electrical energy in an electric field. It is a passive electronic component with two terminals. The effect of a capacitor is known as capacitance. While some capacitance exists between any two electrical conductors in proximity in a circuit, a capacitor is a component designed to add capacitance to a circuit.

## Use / Function

- **Energy Storage:** Storing charge for rapid release (like in a camera flash).
- **Filtering:** Removing noise from power supplies.
- **Tuning:** Used in radio receivers to select specific frequencies.
- **Coupling/Decoupling:** Blocking direct current while allowing alternating current to pass between circuit stages.
- **Timing:** Used with resistors to create precise time delays.

## Operating principle

A capacitor consists of two conductive plates separated by an insulating material called a dielectric.

1.  **Accumulation:** When a voltage is applied, electrons accumulate on one plate (making it negative) and are repelled from the other plate (making it positive).
2.  **Electric Field:** The separation of charges creates an electric field across the dielectric.
3.  **Storage:** This field stores energy. Even when the voltage source is removed, the charges remain on the plates due to their mutual attraction across the dielectric.
4.  **Discharge:** When the plates are connected via a load, the electrons flow back to neutralize the charge, releasing the stored energy.

## How to create it

### Simple Leyden Jar (Early Capacitor)
1.  **Container:** Use a **[Glass](/en/materials/glass)** jar.
2.  **Conductive Layers:** Coat the inside and outside of the jar with **[Metal](/en/materials/metal)** foil (like **[Aluminum](/en/materials/aluminum)** or **[Copper](/en/materials/copper)**).
3.  **Connection:** Attach a wire to the inner foil and another to the outer foil.
4.  **Charging:** Apply a high-voltage static charge to the inner wire.

### Simple Parallel Plate Capacitor
1.  **Plates:** Take two sheets of metal foil.
2.  **Dielectric:** Place a sheet of **[Paper](/en/materials/paper)**, plastic, or thin **[Ceramics](/en/materials/ceramics)** between them.
3.  **Assembly:** Roll or stack them tightly to maximize surface area in a small volume.
4.  **Encapsulation:** Seal the assembly to prevent moisture and damage.

## Materials needed

- **Plates:** **[Aluminum](/en/materials/aluminum)**, **[Copper](/en/materials/copper)**, or **[Silver](/en/materials/silver)** foil.
- **Dielectric:** **[Paper](/en/materials/paper)** (often soaked in oil), Mica, **[Ceramics](/en/materials/ceramics)**, **[Glass](/en/materials/glass)**, or air.
- **Leads:** Conductive wire.

## Variants and improvements

- **Electrolytic Capacitor:** Uses a liquid or gel electrolyte to achieve very high capacitance in a small size (polarized).
- **Ceramic Capacitor:** Small, reliable, used for high-frequency applications.
- **Variable Capacitor:** Allows the user to change the capacitance by moving one set of plates relative to another (used for tuning radios).
- **Supercapacitor:** Extremely high capacitance, bridging the gap between capacitors and batteries.

## Limits and risks

- **Breakdown Voltage:** If the voltage is too high, the dielectric will fail and the capacitor will short-circuit (often explosively).
- **Polarity:** Electrolytic capacitors can explode if connected backwards.
- **Stored Charge:** Large capacitors can hold a lethal charge long after the power is turned off. Always discharge them safely.
- **Leakage:** No dielectric is perfect; capacitors slowly lose their charge over time.
