# Brief description

A greenhouse is a structure with walls and roof made chiefly of transparent material, such as glass, in which plants requiring regulated climatic conditions are grown. It allows for the cultivation of crops outside of their natural season or geographic range by trapping solar energy.

## Use / Function

- **Season Extension**: Allows for earlier planting in spring and later harvesting in autumn.
- **Climate Control**: Protects plants from frost, wind, and excessive rain.
- **Propagation**: Provides an ideal environment for starting seeds and rooting cuttings.
- **Crop Diversification**: Enables the growth of tropical or sensitive plants in colder climates.

## Operating principle

The greenhouse operates on the principle of the "greenhouse effect," combined with physical protection.

1.  **Solar Radiation**: Short-wavelength solar radiation passes through the transparent walls and roof.
2.  **Absorption and Re-radiation**: This radiation is absorbed by the plants, soil, and internal surfaces, which then re-radiate it as long-wavelength infrared radiation (heat).
3.  **Trapping Heat**: The transparent material is mostly opaque to infrared radiation, trapping the heat inside.
4.  **Convection Barrier**: The structure prevents the warm air from escaping through convection and protects against cooling winds.

## How to create it

1.  **Site Selection**: Choose a level site with maximum solar exposure, ideally facing south (in the northern hemisphere).
2.  **Foundation**: Build a sturdy foundation using **[Stone](/en/materials/stone)** or **[Brick](/en/materials/brick)** to prevent sinking and provide thermal mass.
3.  **Frame**: Construct a frame using durable **[Wood](/en/materials/wood)** or metal. The structure must be strong enough to withstand wind and snow loads.
4.  **Glazing**: Cover the frame with **[Glass](/en/materials/glass)** panes or, in more primitive versions, translucent oiled paper or stretched animal membranes.
5.  **Ventilation**: Install operable windows or vents at high and low points to allow for airflow and temperature regulation on hot days.
6.  **Sealing**: Ensure joints are well-sealed to prevent drafts, while still allowing for necessary ventilation.

## Materials needed

- **Frame**: **[Wood](/en/materials/wood)** (cedar or redwood are naturally rot-resistant) or metal.
- **Glazing**: **[Glass](/en/materials/glass)** is best for durability and light transmission.
- **Foundation/Floor**: **[Stone](/en/materials/stone)**, **[Brick](/en/materials/brick)**, or **[Concrete](/en/materials/concrete)**.
- **Fasteners**: Nails, screws, or putty for securing glass.

## Variants and improvements

- **Cold Frame**: A simple, low-to-the-ground box with a transparent lid, acting as a miniature greenhouse for starting seedlings.
- **Walipini (Pit Greenhouse)**: An underground greenhouse that utilizes the earth's thermal mass to maintain stable temperatures, even in very cold climates.
- **Polytunnel**: A large, hoop-shaped structure covered in plastic film; cheaper and easier to build than glass greenhouses.
- **Hydroponic Greenhouse**: Combines greenhouse climate control with **[Hydroponics](/en/knowledge/hydroponics)** for high-efficiency food production.

## Limits and risks

- **Overheating**: Without proper ventilation, temperatures can quickly reach levels that will kill plants.
- **Humidity and Disease**: High humidity can encourage the growth of mold and fungi; requires careful management.
- **Maintenance**: Glass is fragile and requires cleaning to maintain light transmission.
- **Pests**: The sheltered environment can also become a haven for certain pests (like whiteflies or aphids) without natural predators.
