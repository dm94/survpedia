# Brief description

A submarine is a watercraft capable of independent operation underwater. It differs from a submersible, which has more limited underwater capability. Submarines can remain submerged for extended periods and navigate at various depths.

## Use / Function

- **Military:** Stealthy reconnaissance, torpedo attacks, and missile platforms.
- **Scientific Research:** Exploring the ocean floor, marine biology, and underwater geology.
- **Exploration:** Reaching depths inaccessible to divers.
- **Maintenance:** Inspecting underwater cables, pipelines, and structures.

## Operating principle

Submarines operate based on **Archimedes' Principle** and the control of buoyancy:

1.  **Positive Buoyancy:** To float on the surface, the submarine's ballast tanks are filled with air, making the overall density of the vessel less than that of the surrounding water.
2.  **Neutral Buoyancy:** To submerge or maintain a specific depth, water is pumped into the ballast tanks, increasing the vessel's weight until its density equals that of the water.
3.  **Negative Buoyancy:** To sink further, more water is added to the tanks.
4.  **Hydroplanes:** Small "wings" or fins on the hull used to control the angle of descent or ascent while moving.
5.  **Propulsion:** Usually provided by electric motors (powered by batteries when submerged) or diesel engines (on the surface).

## How to create it

Building a safe submarine requires extreme precision and high-quality materials to withstand water pressure:

- **Pressure Hull:** A strong, watertight cylinder (usually steel) that houses the crew and equipment.
- **Ballast Tanks:** Compartments between the inner and outer hulls that can be flooded with water or blown out with compressed air.
- **Sealant:** High-pressure seals for hatches, propeller shafts, and periscopes.
- **Life Support:** Systems to scrub CO2 from the air and provide fresh oxygen (or carry compressed air).

## Materials needed

- **Hull:** **[Steel](/en/materials/steel)** or **[Iron](/en/materials/iron)** (must be thick enough to resist crushing pressure).
- **Windows/Viewports:** Thick **[Glass](/en/materials/glass)** or acrylic (must be pressure-rated).
- **Ballast Control:** **[Pumps](/en/inventions/pump)** and **[Valves](/en/inventions/valve)**.
- **Energy Storage:** **[Battery](/en/inventions/battery)** banks (lead-acid batteries were common in early designs).
- **Propulsion:** **[Electric Motor](/en/inventions/electric-motor)** and **[Internal Combustion Engine](/en/inventions/internal-combustion-engine)**.

## Variants and improvements

- **Human-Powered (Turtle):** Early small submarines propelled by hand-cranked propellers.
- **Diesel-Electric:** Uses diesel engines on the surface to charge batteries for underwater operation.
- **Nuclear-Powered:** Uses a nuclear reactor for near-infinite range and submerged endurance.
- **Bathyscaphe:** Specifically designed for extreme deep-sea exploration (e.g., Challenger Deep).

## Limits and risks

- **Pressure:** Every 10 meters of depth increases pressure by 1 atmosphere. Exceeding the "crush depth" results in catastrophic hull failure.
- **Air Supply:** Limited oxygen and accumulation of CO2 are constant threats.
- **Navigation:** GPS does not work underwater; submarines must rely on sonar and inertial navigation.
- **Entanglement:** Risk of getting caught in nets or underwater structures.
