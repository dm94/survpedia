# Brief description

A biogas digester is an airtight container that facilitates the anaerobic digestion of organic matter (such as animal manure, food scraps, and plant waste) by bacteria. This process produces biogas—a mixture primarily of methane and carbon dioxide—which can be used as a clean fuel for cooking, heating, or lighting.

## Use / Function

- **Fuel Production:** Provides a renewable source of methane for cooking, heating, and power generation.
- **Waste Management:** Hygienically disposes of organic waste and reduces pathogens.
- **Fertilizer Production:** The byproduct (slurry) is a nutrient-rich organic fertilizer.
- **Scale:** Domestic (household size) to Industrial (large-scale farming).

## Operating principle

1.  **Anaerobic Environment:** Organic waste is placed in an airtight tank where oxygen is excluded.
2.  **Hydrolysis:** Bacteria break down complex organic polymers (proteins, fats, carbohydrates) into smaller molecules.
3.  **Acidogenesis and Acetogenesis:** Other bacteria convert these molecules into organic acids and then into acetic acid, hydrogen, and CO2.
4.  **Methanogenesis:** Methanogenic archaea convert the acetic acid and hydrogen into methane (CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2).
5.  **Gas Capture:** The resulting biogas rises to the top of the digester, where it is collected in a gas holder or dome.

## How to create it

### Fixed-Dome Digester (Intermediate)

1.  **Inlet:** A pipe or tank where organic waste mixed with water is introduced.
2.  **Digestion Chamber:** A large, airtight underground tank (usually cylindrical or hemispherical) made of **[Brick](/en/materials/brick)** or **[Concrete](/en/materials/concrete)**.
3.  **Gas Dome:** The upper part of the chamber where gas accumulates. It must be sealed with **[Clay](/en/materials/clay)**, wax, or specialized paint to prevent leaks.
4.  **Outlet:** An overflow tank where the digested slurry (fertilizer) is expelled as new waste is added.
5.  **Gas Pipe:** A **[Plastic](/en/materials/plastic)** or **[Steel](/en/materials/steel)** pipe with a valve to transport the gas to the point of use.

## Materials needed

- **Tank Structure:** **[Brick](/en/materials/brick)**, **[Stone](/en/materials/stone)**, or **[Concrete](/en/materials/concrete)**.
- **Sealing:** **[Clay](/en/materials/clay)**, bitumen, or epoxy resin.
- **Pipes:** **[Plastic](/en/materials/plastic)** (PVC/HDPE) or **[Steel](/en/materials/steel)**.
- **Feedstock:** Animal manure, kitchen waste, crop residues, and **[Water](/en/materials/water)**.

## Variants and improvements

- **Floating-Drum Digester:** Features a movable gas holder (often made of **[Steel](/en/materials/steel)** or fiberglass) that rises and falls with the gas volume, providing constant pressure.
- **Bag/Balloon Digester:** A flexible bag made of UV-resistant plastic or rubber; inexpensive and easy to install but more prone to damage.
- **Continuous vs. Batch:** Continuous digesters are fed daily; batch digesters are filled once and emptied after the gas production stops.

## Limits and risks

- **Temperature:** Bacteria are sensitive; the digester works best in warm climates (above 20°C). Cold weather significantly reduces gas production.
- **Airtightness:** Any leak will lose gas and potentially allow oxygen in, which stops the anaerobic process.
- **Explosion Risk:** Methane is flammable; leaks in enclosed spaces can be dangerous. Keep the digester away from open flames.
- **pH Balance:** Overfeeding with acidic waste (like citrus) can "sour" the digester and kill the bacteria.
